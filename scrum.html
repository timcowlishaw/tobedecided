What is Scrum?

Scrum is a product development methodology that embodies the Agile principles, as well as incorporating it's own Three pillars of Transparency, Adaption and Inspection. Scrum recognises that customer requirements may change, and therefore aims to ensure that the team is as responsive as possible to these changes in requirements.

Scrum in IRFS

Scrum gives us a way to frequently check how we're getting on with a project. It allows us to spot problems quickly (at retrospective) and evaluate progress regularly (at the review). This should give us good visibility of how a project's getting on, and opportunities to decide whether to continue with particular pieces of work (or not).

Return on Investment

The return on investment (ROI) for each user story is calculated from the value assigned to each user story divided by the difficulty on a fibonacci scale

Roles

A scrum team consists of a Product Owner, who is responsible for the Product vision, a Scrum Master, who is responsible for ensuring team follows the Scrum process, and the team - who are everyone else.

Definition of Done

Set of criteria to be applied to each user story to help ensure quality. e.g. Code is peer reviewed

Scrum events

These are all time windowed

  • Sprint Planning
  • Daily Standups
  • Sprint Review
  • Sprint Retrospectives

Scrum Artifacts

There are a number of Artefacts which can be useful for the SCRUM process, including:
  • The Product backlog which outlines the user stories remaining in the product. This is managed and prioritised by the Product Owner.
  • The Sprint backlog which outlines the work the team has committed to deliver in a given sprint. This is decided on by the team in collaboration with the Product Owner
  • The Product and sprint burndown charts which measure and track the progess in delivering the product and the increment to be worked on in a sprint
